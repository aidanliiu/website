Core Features

Overview

AI built for the people who need it most

The idea came from noticing how much of the existing AI landscape is built for tech-savvy users. Tools like ChatGPT assume you know what a prompt is. We wanted to build something for older adults who might benefit the most from AI but find most interfaces overwhelming.

We focused relentlessly on one question: can a 70-year-old who has never used a smartphone understand this immediately?

Design Decisions

Every pixel was decided by asking one question

Can a 70-year-old who has never used a smartphone understand this immediately? That meant large buttons, 20px body text as the floor, high-contrast white cards, and exactly three primary actions on the home screen.

We shipped without a hamburger menu, a buried settings icon, or any gesture-based navigation. All primary actions are reachable in one tap from home.

Challenges

Five things that were harder than expected

01
Getting the conversational tone right
Most LLM outputs feel clinical or overly verbose. We spent weeks prompt-engineering responses to feel warm, patient, and appropriately paced for older adults.
02
App Store review rejections
Two rejections before we got the privacy disclosures and data handling documentation right. Shipping taught me how much work happens outside the code.
03
Testing with real 65+ users
Most design assumptions fail immediately when you watch a real elderly user try your app. We ran four rounds of in-person beta testing to unlearn our own biases.
04
Font size and layout flexibility
Supporting four font sizes (16/20/24/28px) without breaking every layout required systematic design thinking, not ad-hoc fixes.
05
Text-to-speech speed calibration
The default system TTS speed is too fast. We built a three-speed system and let users discover the right speed themselves rather than picking a default.

What I Learned

First time taking something from zero to the App Store

The biggest lesson: design is anticipating confusion before it happens. The second: the gap between "it works" and "it ships" is enormous, and most of that gap has nothing to do with code.

I also learned how to gather qualitative feedback from non-technical users, navigate App Store policy, and think seriously about user acquisition and retention.

01
Shipping teaches you things building doesn't. Review, user responses, and edge cases revealed more than any dev environment could.
02
Design is a user research problem. Every visual decision was downstream of observing real users fail at something obvious to us.
03
Business matters even in a side project. ASO, user acquisition, App Store screenshots determine whether anyone finds what you built.
04
Co-founding is fast and hard. Decisions move faster with two people but require more alignment. Communication is the most important feature.
