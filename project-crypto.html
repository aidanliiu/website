Post-Quantum Cryptography Research

Jul – Aug 2024

Overview

Through the Silicon Valley Cybersecurity Institute (SVCSI) research program, I spent the summer of 2024 diving into post-quantum cryptography — the field focused on developing encryption systems that remain secure even against quantum computers.

With the advent of large-scale quantum computing, many of today's widely-used encryption algorithms (like RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography) would become vulnerable. NIST has already begun standardizing post-quantum replacements, and I wanted to understand how and why.

The research

My research focused on lattice-based cryptographic algorithms, particularly CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium — two of the algorithms NIST selected for standardization. I studied the mathematical foundations of lattice problems (specifically Learning With Errors, or LWE) that make these algorithms computationally hard to break.

I wrote a formal research paper covering the threat model (Shor's algorithm against RSA/ECC), an explanation of lattice-based cryptographic constructions, and an analysis of performance trade-offs between classical and post-quantum schemes.

Key takeaways

The most surprising thing I learned is that the security of lattice-based cryptography doesn't just rely on quantum hardness — it also holds up against classical attacks in ways that other alternatives (like hash-based or code-based schemes) don't as cleanly.

I also came away with a much deeper appreciation for why cryptographic standards take years to finalize. The NIST process involved years of public analysis and attack attempts by the global cryptographic community — that rigor is what gives a standard legitimacy.

Presentation

I presented the research to a panel at SVCSI and received feedback from cybersecurity professionals. The full paper is available to read via Google Drive.

This was my first formal academic research paper, and the process of writing clearly for a technical audience — structuring arguments, citing sources correctly, and editing for concision — was as valuable as the technical content itself.
